About Jellyfish

Jellyfish are among the most ancient and fascinating creatures in the ocean, drifting through Earth‚Äôs waters for over 500 million years‚Äîmaking them older than dinosaurs and even trees. Despite their name, jellyfish are not fish at all; they belong to the phylum Cnidaria, a group of animals that also includes corals and sea anemones.

Their bodies are composed of about 95‚Äì98% water, giving them their translucent, gelatinous form. They lack bones, a heart, or even a brain, yet they have survived for millions of years thanks to specialized structures such as cnidocytes‚Äîtiny stinging cells that help them capture prey and defend against predators.

Found in oceans all around the world, from shallow coastal waters to the deepest seas, jellyfish come in a wide variety of shapes, sizes, and colors. Some are barely visible to the naked eye, while others, like the giant Nomura‚Äôs jellyfish, can grow over two meters in diameter. Their pulsating movements and bioluminescent glow make them one of the most captivating sights in marine ecosystems.

Ecologically, jellyfish play vital roles: they are predators of plankton and small fish, prey for turtles and larger marine species, and recyclers of nutrients in the ocean. However, large blooms of jellyfish can sometimes disrupt human activities by clogging fishing nets, damaging coastal industries, or even affecting power plant operations.

Beyond their ecological significance, jellyfish are also of great scientific interest. Their simple nervous systems, unique proteins such as Green Fluorescent Protein (GFP), and complex venom compositions have inspired research in fields ranging from biotechnology and neuroscience to medicine.
